Proteomic Data Analysis

4.1 Background

In most global proteomic experiments, the main question being asked is: “what protein, or set of proteins, expression level(s) are changing in response to some kind of stimulus (e.g., drug treatment, gene knockout, environmental stressor, etc.)?”

To answer this question, we usually will collect samples (e.g., cultured cells, tissue, plasma, etc.) from one group representing the background proteome (the control) and from another group that has been exposed to our stimulus of interest, measure the protein levels from both groups and compare those levels. However, biology is usually (actually, almost always) very complex and sources of variation can present themselves at every stage of the experiment. For this reason, most experiments will employ replication to try and “reduce” the variation. In other words, if we do not observe differences in protein expression levels between different replicates of the same group, but those same proteins have differences in expression between the different groups, we can be more confident the observed expression change is reflective of a true biological phenomenon.

Luckily for us, statisticians have developed several methods to standardize the comparison between various groups, while controlling for inter-group variation; the one we’ll be focusing on in this tutorial is called the t-test, specifically what’s called a moderated t-test, as employed in the R package limma (https://bioconductor.org/packages/release/bioc/html/limma.html)

Types of t-tests

Within the world of statistics, there a multiple flavors of t-tests, the “appropriate” one to use depending upon your data and experimental design. Those types are:

  • Unpaired T-Test: Also know as an independent samples t-test. Used when two completely separate and distinct groups are being compared (e.g., 3 cell culture plates treated with DMSO and 3 different plates treated with a drug).

    • Assesses whether the difference between the averages of these two groups is larger than you would expect by random chance.

  • Paired T-Test: Used when the observations in one group are directly linked to the observations in the other group (e.g., measuring a patient’s blood pressure before and after medication)

    • A paired t-test is more “powerful” than an unpaired t-test and is useful when noise can vary dramatically between individuals (samples, replicates, subjects, etc.) as each individual acts as their own control.

  • Moderated T-Test: When performing thousands of t-tests simultaneously (like one would do in an omics experiment), some genes will accidentally display unusual variances simply by chance. Through use of an empirical Bayes method, this kind of t-test shrinks individual gene variances towards a pooled, global variance estimate.

    • A moderated t-test prevents false positives and increases statistical power for experiments with low sample sizes.

Refer to the table below for a table detailing the differences between these three t-tests:

Feature Unpaired T-Test Paired T-Test Moderated T-Test
Data Structure Individual groups (e.g., control vs. treated) Dependent pairs (e.g., same person before/after) Thousands of simultaneous variables (e.g., proteins)
Question Is the difference between the two group means significant? Is the mean of the differences significantly different than 0? Is the effect size (e.g., fold-change in expression) for each variable significant?
Variance Handling Analyzes variance between the two independent groups Analyzes the variances within each pair Uses emperical Bayes to “squeeze” or moderate extreme variance estimates
Optimal Use Case A/B testing, clinical trials Repeated measures and matched-pair experimental designs Global proteomics, Bulk RNA-seq, gene expression microarrays
